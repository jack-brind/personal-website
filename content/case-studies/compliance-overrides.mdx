# The problem that was costing us customers

Compliance in staffing software is binary by design — either a candidate is cleared to work, or they aren't. At itris, that meant recruiters couldn't create a placement record until every check was satisfied. For most sectors, this works. For education, it didn't.

Teachers are often placed months before a school year starts. Compliance checks — DBS certificates, right to work documents, qualifications — take time to process, but clients need contracts confirmed early. Recruiters were stuck: they couldn't place, couldn't invoice, and couldn't satisfy clients. So they found workarounds. Messy, audit-unfriendly ones.

One customer told us directly they wouldn't upgrade to the latest version without a solution to this. Their contract was worth over £150k.

# What we actually needed to solve

The temptation was to soften compliance — introduce a grey area between "blocked" and "allowed." We pushed back on that. The whole point of preventative compliance is that it's strict. Blurring the line would erode trust in the system and create liability for our customers.

The real problem wasn't that compliance was too strict. It was that there was no safe mechanism for an authorised person to say _"I know this isn't compliant yet — I'm accountable for that decision."_

# The insight that changed everything

One request kept coming up in customer feedback: a manager should be able to type in a password to unblock a placement. It made sense intuitively, but it was exploitable — if anyone saw the password, they could use it indefinitely with no trace.

That's when I started thinking about how modern apps handle sensitive one-time authorisation: two-factor authentication. What if, instead of a static password, a privileged user could generate a time-limited, single-use code? The compliance block would still exist. The candidate would still show as non-compliant. But an authorised person could issue a code, and the override would be logged — who issued it, when, and for which placement.

It threads the needle: compliance teams keep their oversight, recruiters get unblocked, and there's a clear audit trail if anything is ever questioned.

# Three decisions that shaped the solution

1. Non-compliance stays visible: Overriding the block doesn't mark the candidate as compliant. They're still flagged in compliance reports. The override is a documented exception, not a status change. This was important for our customers' regulatory obligations.
2. Code generation is privileged: Only designated users can generate override codes. This keeps the accountability chain clear — a recruiter can't unblock themselves; they need sign-off from someone with authority.
3. Audit trail is first-class: Every override is logged with the issuer, timestamp, and context. This wasn't an afterthought — it was central to the pitch internally. Without it, the feature would have created liability; with it, it reduced it.

# What happened

Within a month of release, the customer who had refused to upgrade came on board. £150k contract, directly attributable to this feature. We were also confident it would improve retention more broadly — this wasn't an edge case request; it was a pattern across multiple customers in education and similar sectors.

# What I'd do differently

I'd have pushed to define the audit trail UI earlier in the process. We got it right, but it was a late conversation — and for a feature where accountability is the whole point, the way that log is surfaced to compliance managers deserved more design attention than we gave it at the time.
